Untiled

Un-tiled is a novelty pick and place machine capable of plotting (and animating) low resolution binary images. It works by flipping a grid of multi-colred tiles one by one in order to form a complete picture. The process begins with a gantry system capable of moving a toolhead to the location of a tile that needs to be flipped. From there, the toolhead is lowerd so that it can pick up the tile using a suction nozzel. The tile is then droped into the "flipper", a gravity fed side which flips the tile to the side with the opposite color. Finaly, the tile is retuend to its spot in the grid and the machine moves on to the next tile.

Untiled was developed by a 5-person team as part of the Olin 2023 Principal of Integrated Engineering class. My most significat contibutions included the design, prototpying, and manufacturin of the x-axis gantry belt system and flipper.

In order to complete each tile-flip in the shortes time possible, the design of the gantry was optemized for speed. This is evident in the "laser-cutter" inspired gantry design. The team elected for a cartesian drive system due to its simplicity and compatiblity with our system architecutre. I was resposible for building out the x-axis motion system which consisted of a motor mount and belt tensioner along with the additional stricutre to mount the x-axis cross beam to the y-xais linear rails.

One major learning opportunity I had when developing this subassebly was how to design for compatability and integration with the other subsystems. In addition to frequent communication with the other memebers of the team, parametric modeling helped my design to be eaily adaptable and meet the dynamic requirements of the other sub-systems.

I also spent significant time developing the passive tile flipper and getting it to function with perfect reliablity.

Despite the team's intial cncerns with the idea, the physical flipping of the tiles turned out to be the easy part and I was able to move from an idea to a wokring prototpe in just a few hours. The challenge was figuring out how to allign the tiles after each flip so that they coudl be picked up again while minimizing possitional inaccuracies. All of the remaning prototypes targeted a different potential failuremode that would emerge during testing.

The final design was highly sucesfull and contributed to the machine's ability to opperate contiusously for hours without human intervention.
